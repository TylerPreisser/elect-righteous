# DEEP INVESTIGATION: 2026 Kansas Constitutional Amendments
## Compiled: March 31, 2026

# AMENDMENT 1: Kansas Supreme Court Election Amendment
## SCR 1611 -- On the AUGUST 4, 2026 PRIMARY BALLOT

**This is arguably the most consequential item on the 2026 Kansas ballot.** It appears on the primary ballot -- meaning dramatically lower turnout will decide the fate of Kansas's judicial system. For context, the 2022 abortion amendment was also placed on an August primary ballot, and that election saw unprecedented primary turnout precisely because of the amendment.

---

## 1. EXACT BALLOT LANGUAGE

The amendment is placed on the ballot via **Senate Concurrent Resolution 1611** (SCR 1611).

**Explanatory Statement (what voters will see):**

> "This amendment gives the voters the right to elect the justices of the Kansas supreme court. The justices shall serve terms of six years, with the elections of justice positions 1, 2 and 3 to occur in 2028, positions 4 and 5 to occur in 2030 and positions 6 and 7 to occur in 2032, and every six years thereafter. The rules applicable for such elections and the designation of position numbers shall be provided by law. Any vacancy on the court for an unexpired term shall be filled at an election as provided by law."

**Key detail in the ballot language**: The phrase "the rules applicable for such elections and the designation of position numbers shall be provided by law" means the Legislature -- not the constitution -- would control the specifics of how these elections work. This gives future legislators enormous discretion over the mechanics.

**Source**: [Kansas Legislature SCR 1611 Summary](https://www.kslegislature.gov/li/b2025_26/measures/documents/summary_scr_1611_2025)

---

## 2. WHAT IT WOULD CHANGE

### THE CURRENT SYSTEM (Merit-Based / "Missouri Plan")

Kansas's current merit-based selection system was established by constitutional amendment in **1958**. Here is how it works:

1. **A vacancy opens** on the Kansas Supreme Court (retirement, death, resignation).
2. **The Kansas Supreme Court Nominating Commission** reviews applications. The commission has **nine members**:
   - One lawyer elected by Kansas Bar Association members from each of the state's four congressional districts (4 lawyers)
   - One non-lawyer appointed by the Governor from each of the state's four congressional districts (4 non-lawyers)
   - One additional lawyer who serves as the commission's chair, elected by the other lawyer members
3. **The commission selects three finalists** and forwards them to the Governor.
4. **The Governor picks one** of the three nominees within 60 days.
5. **Retention elections**: After the justice has served at least one year, they face an uncontested yes/no retention vote at the next general election. Voters decide: "Shall Justice [Name] be retained?" If a majority votes "no," the seat becomes vacant and the cycle begins again. Justices serve **six-year terms** between retention votes.

**Strengths of this system (as argued by supporters)**:
- Justices are evaluated on qualifications, not fundraising ability
- Insulates the judiciary from partisan politics
- Has produced a respected, independent court for nearly 70 years
- Justices don't owe their seats to donors or party bosses

**Criticisms of this system (as argued by opponents)**:
- The Bar Association (lawyers) controls 5 of 9 commission seats, giving the legal profession outsized influence
- The public has no direct voice in initial selection -- only in retention
- Retention elections are almost never competitive (justices are almost always retained)
- The system concentrates power in a small elite group

### THE PROPOSED SYSTEM (Direct Election)

If the amendment passes:

1. **All seven Supreme Court positions** would be filled by **direct popular election** on staggered schedules:
   - Positions 1, 2, and 3: Elected **November 2028**
   - Positions 4 and 5: Elected **November 2030**
   - Positions 6 and 7: Elected **November 2032**
   - All positions: Every **six years** thereafter
2. **The Supreme Court Nominating Commission would be abolished.**
3. **Justices and candidates** would be allowed to **make political contributions and participate in political campaigns** -- a dramatic shift from current norms of judicial neutrality.
4. **Vacancies** for unexpired terms would be "filled at an election as provided by law."
5. **Critical detail**: The specific rules for these elections -- partisan vs. nonpartisan, filing requirements, campaign finance rules, district vs. statewide, primary mechanics -- **are not specified in the amendment itself**. They would be "provided by law," meaning the Legislature would write the rules later.

**What this means in practice**: Kansas would join states like Texas, Pennsylvania, and Ohio where Supreme Court seats are contested political races. Based on the experience of those states, this would mean:
- Candidates would need to raise millions of dollars for statewide campaigns
- Political parties, PACs, and special interest groups would pour money into judicial races
- Justices would have political allegiances and campaign donors they might feel beholden to
- Judicial campaigns would feature attack ads, opposition research, and partisan messaging

**Sources**: [Ballotpedia - Kansas Elections for Supreme Court Justices Amendment (August 2026)](https://ballotpedia.org/Kansas_Elections_for_Supreme_Court_Justices_Amendment_(August_2026)); [Kansas Legislature SCR 1611](https://kslegislature.gov/li/b2025_26/measures/scr1611/)

## 3. WHO PUSHED THIS THROUGH THE LEGISLATURE

### Senate Vote (March 6, 2025): PASSED 27-13
- Required 27 votes (two-thirds of 40 senators). Got exactly the minimum.
- **Four Republicans joined all Democrats in voting NO.**
- In the House, the four Republican dissenters were identified: Rep. Jesse Borjon (Topeka), Rep. Angel Roeser (Manhattan), Rep. Mark Schreiber (Emporia), and Rep. Sean Willcott (Holton).
- The Senate Republican dissenters' specific names are available on the [LegiScan roll call page](https://legiscan.com/KS/rollcall/SCR1611/id/1507336).

### House Vote (March 19, 2025): PASSED 84-40
- Required 84 votes (two-thirds of 125 House members). Got exactly the minimum.
- The House declared an **emergency** to take final action without waiting the customary day between debate and vote.
- Four House Republicans also crossed party lines to vote No.

### Party-Line Analysis
- The vote was overwhelmingly party-line on both sides. Every Democrat in both chambers voted No. Approximately **93% of Republicans voted Yes** (with a small handful of dissenters in each chamber).
- This tracks with national data: Republican legislators supported similar judicial election amendments across 16 states at an average of 99.7% support.

### Senate President Ty Masterson's Role
Masterson, an Andover Republican, was the driving force behind SCR 1611 in the Senate. His key quote:

> "Rather than a system that consolidates authority in the hands of an elite society of super voters, it's time to restore the power to all Kansans."

Masterson framed the amendment as a populist measure returning power to voters, though critics note the amendment would actually increase the power of money in judicial selection.

### Governor Kelly's Role
**Governor Laura Kelly had NO say in this.** Under the Kansas Constitution, **constitutional amendments bypass the governor entirely.** A concurrent resolution proposing a constitutional amendment requires a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers but does NOT go to the governor for signature or veto. The governor cannot block it. Kelly, a Democrat, almost certainly would have vetoed it if she could have.

**Sources**: [Kansas Reflector - Senate Adopts Plan](https://kansasreflector.com/2025/03/07/kansas-senate-adopts-plan-to-elect-supreme-court-justices-a-step-toward-overturning-abortion-rights/); [Kansas Reflector - House Endorses Plan](https://kansasreflector.com/2025/03/19/kansas-house-endorses-plan-to-elect-supreme-court-justices-placing-question-on-august-2026-ballot/); [Kansas Legislature SCR 1611 Supplemental Note (PDF)](https://kslegislature.gov/li/b2025_26/measures/documents/supp_note_scr1611_01_0000.pdf)

## 5. WHO OPPOSES IT AND WHY

### Kansas Bar Association
The KBA officially opposed the amendment. Their position is straightforward: the merit system produces qualified, independent judges. They note that the current system was specifically designed to prevent political corruption of the judiciary.

### ACLU of Kansas
The ACLU of Kansas called the amendment:
> "A blatant attack by the legislators on our justices, and it's part of a decades-long pattern of politicians attempting to punish the judicial branch for issuing decisions on education and reproductive freedom that they disagree with."

The ACLU also labeled the amendment the **"Kobach Power Grab,"** arguing Kobach's own admission that the current court makes it hard for him to win cases reveals the true motivation.

**Source**: [ACLU of Kansas - SCR 1611](https://www.aclukansas.org/legislation/scr-1611-judicial-selection-amendment/); [ACLU of Kansas - Stop the Kobach Power Grab](https://www.aclukansas.org/publications/stop-kobach-power-grab/)

### Kansas Association of School Boards

**Why would a school board group care about judicial selection?** This is a critical question with a clear answer: **The Kansas Supreme Court has been the sole enforcer of the constitutional mandate to fund public schools.**

The *Gannon v. State* case (2010-2024) was a **14-year legal battle** in which the Kansas Supreme Court repeatedly ruled that the Legislature was violating Article 6 of the Kansas Constitution by underfunding K-12 public schools. The court ordered the Legislature to increase funding multiple times, ultimately resulting in billions of additional dollars for Kansas schools. The court did not release jurisdiction over the case until February 2024.

Mark Tallman of the Kansas Association of School Boards noted that **four major school funding lawsuits over 60 years** have relied on the Supreme Court to enforce the constitutional mandate for adequate school funding. If justices had to run for election with campaign donations from groups that oppose school funding increases, the court might no longer serve as a check on legislative underfunding.

In short: **school boards fear that elected justices beholden to anti-tax donors would stop enforcing the constitutional right to adequate school funding.**

**Source**: [KCUR - Rivals Launch Fight](https://www.kcur.org/politics-elections-and-government/2025-08-10/rivals-launch-fight-over-kansas-constitutional-amendment-to-elect-supreme-court-justices)

## 6. THE REAL MOTIVATION

### Connection to the 2019 Abortion Decision (Hodes & Nauser v. Schmidt)

**This is, in the assessment of virtually all analysts and opponents, the single biggest motivating factor.**

In April 2019, the Kansas Supreme Court ruled 6-1 in *Hodes & Nauser v. Schmidt* that Section 1 of the Kansas Bill of Rights protects a **fundamental right to personal autonomy**, which includes the right to decide whether to continue a pregnancy. The court applied **strict scrutiny** -- the highest standard of judicial review -- meaning any law restricting abortion must serve a compelling government interest and be narrowly tailored.

This decision made Kansas one of the strongest state-level protections for abortion rights in the country, even after Dobbs v. Jackson overturned Roe v. Wade federally in 2022.

In July 2024, the Kansas Supreme Court **reaffirmed and expanded** this ruling, striking down a state law banning a common second-trimester abortion procedure (dilation and evacuation).

**Sources**: [NPR - Kansas Supreme Court Rules Constitution Protects Abortion Rights](https://www.npr.org/2019/04/26/717449336/kansas-supreme-court-rules-state-constitution-protects-right-to-abortion); [Kansas Reflector - Supreme Court Strikes Down Abortion Law](https://kansasreflector.com/2024/07/05/kansas-supreme-court-strikes-down-state-law-banning-second-trimester-abortion-procedure/)

### Connection to the Failed 2022 Abortion Amendment

In August 2022, Kansas voters **overwhelmingly rejected** a constitutional amendment (the "Value Them Both" amendment) that would have removed abortion protections from the Kansas Constitution. The vote was approximately **59% No to 41% Yes** -- an 18-point landslide in a traditionally red state, with record primary turnout.

The judicial selection amendment can be understood as **Plan B** for opponents of abortion rights in Kansas. If you can't change the constitution directly (voters rejected that in 2022), you change the judges who interpret the constitution. By replacing merit-selected justices with elected ones, and by timing the first elections for 2028-2032, conservative groups could potentially replace the current court with justices who would overturn *Hodes & Nauser*.

The Kansas Reflector headlined its coverage of the Senate vote: **"Kansas Senate adopts plan to elect Supreme Court justices, a step toward overturning abortion rights."**

**Source**: [Kansas Reflector - Senate Adopts Plan](https://kansasreflector.com/2025/03/07/kansas-senate-adopts-plan-to-elect-supreme-court-justices-a-step-toward-overturning-abortion-rights/); [Kansas Reflector - Voters Defeat Abortion Amendment](https://kansasreflector.com/2022/08/02/kansas-voters-defeat-abortion-amendment-in-unexpected-landslide-1/)

### Connection to School Funding (Gannon v. State)

The *Gannon v. State* litigation (2010-2024) saw the Kansas Supreme Court repeatedly order the Legislature to increase K-12 funding to meet the constitutional mandate of "suitable provision for finance of the educational interests of the state." The case spanned seven separate opinions over 14 years and resulted in billions in additional school funding. The Legislature was deeply frustrated by a court that kept telling it to spend more money.

Earlier school funding cases (*Montoy v. State*, 2003-2006) had a similar dynamic.

**Source**: [KLRD - Gannon v. State Overview and Timeline](https://klrd.gov/2024/04/23/gannon-v-state-overview-and-timeline/)

### The Pattern

The Kansas Reflector, ACLU, and multiple opposition groups have identified a **decades-long pattern**:
1. The Supreme Court issues a ruling conservatives dislike (abortion, school funding, voting rights)
2. The Legislature attempts to change the judicial selection system
3. Various proposals have been introduced over the years; SCR 1611 is the one that finally passed

AG Kobach's own candid statement that the court's makeup makes it "very difficult" for him to win cases is perhaps the clearest evidence of motivation.

## 7. NATIONAL CONTEXT: States That Elect Judges

### The Evidence on Judicial Elections and Money

**22 states** use some form of partisan or nonpartisan election for supreme court justices. The evidence from these states is extensive and concerning:

**Campaign Spending Explosion**:
- State supreme court elections nationally attracted **more than $100 million** in the 2021-22 cycle alone -- nearly double any prior midterm, inflation-adjusted.
- The 2023 Pennsylvania Supreme Court race saw at least **$22 million** in spending, making it one of the most expensive judicial elections in American history.
- Pennsylvania's 2025 retention races (traditionally low-profile) saw more than **$13 million** in spending.
- In Texas, a single PAC spent almost **$2 million** on state supreme court races.

**Impact on Judicial Independence**:
- Research shows fundraising pressures influence justices' decision-making, whether consciously or unconsciously.
- A study found law firms that donated **$400 or more** to judicial campaigns in North Carolina won **53%** of their cases, compared to 48% for firms giving less than $400.
- *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) opened the door to unlimited "independent expenditures" in judicial races, dramatically increasing outside spending.

**What Former Judges Say**:
- Members of the Texas judiciary, including Justice Bob Gammage and Chief Justice John Hill, have expressed dissatisfaction with the rising tide of campaign contributions.
- Former Kansas Chief Justice Lawton Nuss said he would not have sought the bench if he had to campaign and fundraise.

**Sources**: [Brennan Center - Money in Judicial Elections](https://www.brennancenter.org/issues/strengthen-our-courts/promote-fair-courts/money-judicial-elections); [Brennan Center - Politics of Judicial Elections 2021-22](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/politics-judicial-elections-2021-2022); [Richmond Public Interest Law Review - Impartial Justice](https://pilr.richmond.edu/2025/02/17/impartial-justice-money-in-judicial-electoral-campaigns-and-the-impacts-on-the-independence-of-state-courts/)

## 9. HISTORICAL CONTEXT: The 1958 Amendment

### The "Triple Play" Scandal

Kansas's current merit selection system exists because of political corruption. In the 1950s:

1. **Governor Fred Hall** lost his bid for reelection in the 1956 Republican primary.
2. Hall then orchestrated the infamous **"Triple Play"**:
   - He convinced Kansas Supreme Court Chief Justice William Smith to resign
   - Hall then resigned as governor himself
   - His Lieutenant Governor briefly became governor and **immediately appointed Hall to the Supreme Court**
3. This blatant manipulation of the system to secure a lifetime judicial appointment sparked **public outrage**.
4. In response, the Kansas Legislature proposed a constitutional amendment creating the merit-based nominating commission system.
5. **Kansas voters approved the amendment in 1958**, specifically to prevent politicians from using judicial appointments as political spoils.

The current system has been in place for **68 years** and was born from the same concern opponents raise today: the danger of political manipulation of the judiciary.

**Source**: [Kansas Information Network - 2026 Constitutional Amendment](https://www.kansasinformationnetwork.com/2026-Kansas-Constitutional-Amendment)

## 10. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### Why August Instead of November?

Placing the amendment on the **August 4 primary ballot** instead of the **November 3 general election** is a deliberate strategic choice. Primary elections have dramatically lower turnout, especially in non-presidential years. Lower turnout generally favors motivated partisan bases over the general electorate.

However, this strategy **backfired spectacularly in 2022** when the abortion amendment was placed on the August primary ballot. That election saw record primary turnout driven by motivated opposition voters.

### The Vagueness Problem

Retired Justice Carol Beier highlighted a critical weakness: the amendment is **exceedingly vague** on the mechanics of judicial elections. Key questions left unanswered:
- Partisan or nonpartisan elections?
- How are candidates nominated -- party primaries, nominating conventions, filing fees?
- What campaign finance rules apply?
- How are judicial districts drawn, or are races statewide?
- What happens during the transition -- do current justices have to run immediately?

All of these details would be **"provided by law"** -- meaning the same Legislature that passed the amendment would write the rules. As Beier asked: *"Do you trust them?"*

### The Dark Money Irony

Supporters frame the amendment as giving "power to the people" and taking it away from "lawyers in smoke-filled back rooms." Yet the primary financial supporter of the "Yes" campaign is **Kansans for Democracy**, a dark money nonprofit that does not disclose its donors. The irony of fighting for "transparency" with anonymous money has not been lost on critics.

# AMENDMENT 2: Kansas Citizenship Voting Requirement
## HCR 5004 -- On the NOVEMBER 3, 2026 GENERAL ELECTION BALLOT

---

## 1. EXACT BALLOT TEXT

The amendment is placed on the ballot via **House Concurrent Resolution 5004** (HCR 5004).

**Amendment language**: The amendment would change the Kansas Constitution to read: "No person shall be deemed a qualified elector unless such person: (1) Is a citizen of the United States..."

**Explanatory Statement**:
> "This amendment would clarify that only a person who is a citizen of the United States is eligible to vote in this state."

**Source**: [Ballotpedia - Kansas Citizenship Voting Requirement Amendment (2026)](https://ballotpedia.org/Kansas_Citizenship_Voting_Requirement_Amendment_(2026))

---

## 2. CURRENT LAW: Is Non-Citizen Voting Already Illegal?

**Yes. It is already illegal for non-citizens to vote in Kansas.** This is true at multiple levels:

- **Federal law**: The Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 makes it a federal crime for non-citizens to vote in federal elections.
- **Kansas state law**: Kansas statutes already require U.S. citizenship to register to vote.
- **Kansas Constitution (current)**: Already lists citizenship as a qualification for electors.

**So what does this amendment actually change legally?**

The amendment changes the constitutional language from an **inclusionary** framing ("every citizen who meets these criteria may vote") to an **exclusionary** framing ("only citizens may vote"). Critics argue this is a substantive shift in constitutional tone that could be weaponized to justify additional voter restrictions. Supporters say it simply "clarifies" existing requirements.

The ACLU of Kansas argues the amendment "does nothing to strengthen our Constitution" and "appears to do nothing at all legally" but could "be weaponized to justify a return to restrictive voter registration laws."

**Source**: [ACLU of Kansas - HCR 5004](https://www.aclukansas.org/en/legislation/hcr-5004-state-citizen-voter)

---

## 3. EVIDENCE: How Many Non-Citizen Voting Cases in Kansas?

### Documented Cases

The evidence of non-citizen voting in Kansas is **extremely thin**:

- **The Kobach Era (2011-2018)**: When Kris Kobach was Kansas Secretary of State, he aggressively pursued voter fraud. In litigation over his proof-of-citizenship law, **fewer than 40 non-citizens were found to have registered to vote over a 20-year period** -- out of millions of registrations.

- **Recent Cases (2025)**: Kansas officials announced indictments of **two people** for fraudulent voting, with a third indictment coming and 10 people being examined. The most high-profile case involves **Coldwater Mayor Joe Ceballos**, a legal permanent resident from Mexico who cast ballots in three elections between 2022 and 2024.

- **National Context**: The Heritage Foundation -- which actively advocates for strict voting requirements -- has documented only **68 cases** of non-citizen voting **nationwide since the 1980s**, out of over **one billion** ballots cast.

- **A 2025 review** by the Center for Election Innovation & Research found that claims of widespread non-citizen voting have not materialized.

### The Kobach Proof-of-Citizenship Precedent

This amendment connects directly to Kobach's earlier efforts. As Kansas Secretary of State:

1. **2013**: Kobach implemented a law requiring **documentary proof of citizenship** (birth certificate or passport) to register to vote.
2. **2016**: A federal appeals court temporarily blocked the law, calling it "a mass denial of a fundamental constitutional right."
3. **2018**: A federal judge **struck down the law entirely**, finding:
   - It violated the National Voter Registration Act
   - It violated the U.S. Constitution
   - There was "no credible evidence" for the claim that voter fraud was rampant
   - The law had blocked **more than 35,000 Kansans** from registering to vote
4. **The judge sanctioned Kobach personally**, ordering him to complete additional continuing legal education courses.

The constitutional amendment could provide legal cover for future attempts to impose proof-of-citizenship requirements that were previously struck down under federal law.

## 5. WHO SUPPORTS IT

- **Kansas Legislature**: Near-unanimous bipartisan support (90-28 House, 37-3 Senate)

- **Kansas Attorney General Kris Kobach**: Who has made voter integrity a signature issue throughout his career
- **Conservative organizations**: Kansas Policy Institute, Kansas Republican Party
- **National alignment**: Part of a nationwide trend of states codifying citizenship requirements

## 6. WHO OPPOSES IT

Opposition is **limited and largely organizational** rather than legislative:

### ACLU of Kansas

Rashane Hamby, ACLU-KS director of policy and research:
> "The language in HCR 5004 could be weaponized to justify a return to restrictive voter registration laws, as it explicitly conditions voter eligibility on U.S. citizenship, despite no evidence of noncitizen voting in Kansas elections. Furthermore, the amendment's reference to 'laws of this state relating to voting for presidential electors' could allow legislators to tighten residency requirements or impose further barriers under the pretense of aligning with federal law."

### League of Women Voters of Kansas
Opposed the measure on the grounds that it is unnecessary and could cause confusion.

### Loud Light Civic Action

Davis Hammet, executive director:
> "This amendment does nothing to strengthen our Constitution, it appears to do nothing at all legally, yet it will cause confusion, inflame tensions, and distract from important conversations."

### Language Concern

The ACLU notes the amendment changes the Kansas Constitution from language that **promotes inclusion** ("everyone who fits the following criteria may vote") to language that **promotes exclusion** ("only those who fit these criteria may vote"). This shift in constitutional framing, they argue, could open the door to more aggressive voter eligibility challenges.

## 7. 2024 PRECEDENT: Eight States Passed Similar Measures

In 2024, **eight states** passed virtually identical constitutional amendments banning non-citizen voting. **All eight passed**, most by wide margins:

| State | Yes Vote | Margin |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Idaho | 64.8% | +29.6 |
| Iowa | ~76% | +52 |
| Kentucky | ~62% | +24 |
| Missouri | ~68% | +36 |
| North Carolina | 77.5% | +55 |
| Oklahoma | ~80%+ | +60+ |
| South Carolina | Approved (exact % not confirmed) | Large margin |
| Wisconsin | ~70% | +40 |

**Key facts about the 2024 measures**:
- From 2018-2025, voters have decided on **15 ballot measures** related to citizenship voting requirements. **All 15 passed.**
- Republican legislators supported these measures at an average of **99.7%**.
- Democratic support varied widely, averaging **43.1%** and ranging from 0% (Missouri, Oklahoma, Wisconsin) to 100% (Alabama 2020, Iowa 2024).
- No state that has put a citizenship voting requirement on the ballot has ever seen it fail.

**Based on this precedent, the Kansas citizenship amendment is virtually certain to pass.** The only question is the margin.

## 8. THE KOBACH CONNECTION

This amendment is deeply connected to Kobach's career-long crusade against what he claims is widespread voter fraud:

1. **As Kansas Secretary of State (2011-2019)**: Implemented documentary proof of citizenship; it was struck down by federal courts and he was personally sanctioned.
2. **As vice chair of Trump's Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity (2017-2018)**: The commission was disbanded after failing to find evidence of widespread voter fraud.
3. **As Kansas Attorney General (2023-present)**: Announced criminal charges related to non-citizen voting; total cases involve a handful of individuals.
4. **As HCR 5004 supporter**: Backs the constitutional amendment that would enshrine citizenship requirements at the constitutional level, potentially providing future legal cover for proof-of-citizenship registration requirements that courts have previously blocked.

The pattern is consistent: Kobach has spent 15+ years building a political brand around the claim that non-citizen voting is a serious problem, despite courts repeatedly finding no evidence to support that claim.

## 10. BOTTOM LINE ASSESSMENT

### Amendment 1 (Supreme Court Elections) -- GENUINELY CONTESTED

This is the real fight. The evidence strongly suggests the amendment is motivated primarily by frustration with the court's rulings on abortion rights and school funding, not by principled concerns about democratic participation. The amendment would fundamentally transform Kansas's judiciary from an independent institution insulated from political pressure into a branch subject to the same campaign financing, partisan messaging, and special interest influence that characterizes legislative and executive elections.

The August primary placement is a strategic choice designed to minimize turnout -- but that same strategy failed badly with the 2022 abortion amendment. Both sides are preparing for a multi-million-dollar campaign.

**Key factors to watch**:
- Total spending by both sides
- Whether the opposition can replicate the 2022 turnout surge
- Whether the "voters not lawyers" framing or the "justice is not for sale" framing resonates more
- The impact of $802,000+ in dark money advertising from Kansans for Democracy
- Whether the foreign donor lawsuit affects KCF's ability to spend on opposition

### Amendment 2 (Citizenship Voting) -- WILL ALMOST CERTAINLY PASS

This amendment is largely symbolic, as non-citizen voting is already illegal in Kansas. It has overwhelming bipartisan legislative support and follows an unbroken national trend of similar measures passing easily. The real question is whether the constitutional language change opens the door to future voter restriction legislation, which is what opponents fear.

The 90-28 House vote and 37-3 Senate vote -- with significant Democratic support -- indicate this is not a close political question. It will likely pass by a wide margin in November.
