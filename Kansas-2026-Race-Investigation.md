# PART ONE: GOVERNOR -- DEMOCRATIC PRIMARY (August 4, 2026)

The Democratic field has three candidates competing for the right to succeed term-limited Gov. Laura Kelly. The two frontrunners are both Johnson County state senators; the third is a Lawrence author/personal trainer with no political experience. A January 2026 poll (commissioned by Holscher's campaign, conducted by Public Policy Polling) showed **58% of Democratic voters undecided**, with Holscher at 33% and Corson at 9% among those who expressed a preference. Name recognition is the dominant factor -- 68% couldn't form an opinion on Holscher, and 79% couldn't on Corson.

# PART TWO: SECRETARY OF STATE RACE (Open Seat)

**Why this race matters**: The Kansas Secretary of State administers elections, oversees voter registration, manages business filings, and certifies election results. Incumbent Scott Schwab (R) is vacating the seat to run for governor. In the post-2020 era, Secretary of State races have become ground zero for election administration fights. Kansas's previous SOS, Kris Kobach, turned the office into a national symbol of voter suppression efforts before losing the 2018 governor's race.

**Primary dates**: August 4, 2026
**General election**: November 3, 2026

### Legislative Record -- Election Bills

As Chairman of the House Elections Committee, Proctor is the architect of Kansas's 2026 election legislation package:

**HB 2437 -- The SAVE Kansas Act** (passed House 80-43):
- Cross-references driver's license records and voter rolls against the federal SAVE (Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements) database twice per year
- Mandates state agencies share personal information (including Social Security numbers) with the Secretary of State's Office
- Restricts voter registration websites to .gov domains or state-approved sites
- Requires county elections officials to remove voters when funeral homes publish obituaries
- Proctor's framing: "Do I believe that there is a vast conspiracy for noncitizens to vote in the state of Kansas? No. The point is that we can tell people with confidence, no, we know noncitizens aren't voting because we're scrubbing the voter rolls."

**HB 2569 -- Advanced Voting Restrictions**:
- Secretary of State must monitor signature verification lawsuits
- If any judge invalidates a signature requirement, voters without a specific excuse (out of state, sickness, disability, religious belief) would be **forbidden from advance voting by mail**

# PART THREE: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

## Governor's Race -- Democratic Primary Dynamics

The Corson vs. Holscher race is the real contest. It breaks down along these lines:

**Establishment vs. Grassroots**: Corson has the governor, the former governor (Sebelius), the lt. governor, and a massive fundraising operation. Holscher has Sharice Davids, Former Gov. Carlin, and a "fighter for the people" brand. Corson is the establishment pick; Holscher is positioning as the insurgent.

**Tone**: Corson runs a positive, centrist campaign designed to appeal in a general election. Holscher runs a combative campaign that names enemies (Brownback, MAGA, billionaire stadium owners). In a primary where Democratic voters are angry about Trump's second term, Holscher's "fighter" brand may resonate more than Corson's "common ground" pitch.

**Money vs. Message**: Corson has a 2.3:1 fundraising advantage. In a low-turnout August primary, money buys name recognition -- and both candidates desperately need it (58% undecided in January).

**The Kelly Factor**: Kelly's endorsement is a double-edged sword. It gives Corson institutional credibility but also tags him as the "anointed" candidate in a cycle where anti-establishment sentiment runs high. The revelation that Kelly's staff tried to clear the field for Chris Mann (not even Corson) makes the endorsement look more transactional than organic.

## Secretary of State Race

**Republican primary**: Proctor wins. He has the money (14:1 fundraising advantage), the legislative record (authored the election bills), the resume, and the message that Republican primary voters want to hear.

**Democratic primary**: Day is the likely nominee. She has legislative experience and genuine election administration credentials.

**General election**: This will be a direct confrontation between Proctor's "election integrity" framework (voter roll purges, noncitizen verification, mail ballot restrictions) and Day's "election access" framework (voter education, accessibility, modernization). Given Kansas's Republican lean in non-presidential years, Proctor would be the favorite, but the office's administrative nature and Day's centrist positioning could make it competitive in Johnson County-heavy turnout scenarios.
