## 5. SECRETARY OF STATE RECORD (2019-Present)

### Election Administration

Schwab has consistently maintained that Kansas elections are **secure, fair, and accurate**. This has been his defining position as Secretary of State, often putting him at odds with conspiracy-minded elements of his own party.

### Key Accomplishments (Per His Office)

1. **Voter ID Implementation:** Led the effort in the Kansas House to enact voter ID into law. The Secure and Fair Elections (SAFE) Act was passed and signed in 2011 under his chairmanship of the Elections Committee. Kansas served as a national model for voter photo ID.

2. **Election Security Legislation (2022):** Proposed and championed a bill signed into law that:
   - Prohibited counties from using voting equipment with internet capability
   - Required the Secretary of State to certify electronic pollbooks
   - Described by Schwab as "one of the most significant election reform measures since Kansas adopted Voter ID in 2011"

3. **SAVE Kansas Act (HB 2437):** Applauded passage, stating "This legislation reflects our commitment to maintaining accurate voter rolls while protecting the rights of every eligible Kansas voter for decades to come."

4. **NASS Leadership:** Appointed to NASS Executive Board in 2020; President-Elect in 2022; **President of NASS in 2023** -- the fifth Kansan to serve as president and first since Ron Thornburgh in 2002.

5. **Kansas Top 10 Election Integrity (2021):** Kansas was named to a Top 10 list for election integrity during Schwab's tenure.

6. **Federal Database Access (SAVE System):** In 2025, Schwab made Kansas one of the first states to commit to using the federal SAVE database to confirm U.S. citizenship of registered voters, earning praise from President Trump.

7. **Missouri-Kansas Voter Data Sharing (November 2025):** Reached agreement with Missouri to share voter registration details of approximately 6 million people to improve voter roll accuracy.

### Rejected 2020 Election Conspiracies

Schwab was a notable voice of reason among Republican secretaries of state after the 2020 election:

**Key Quote:** "Was there organized, rigged fraud that changed the outcome of the election? I have seen no evidence of that."

- Maintained there was no issue with voter fraud or election security in Kansas
- This position was **directly at odds with President Trump's claims** at the time
- Dismissed baseless fraud theories "at least as a possibility in Kansas elections"
- Defended the use of ballot drop boxes, which Trump and other Republicans attacked
- Kansas Reflector headline from November 2020: "As other Republicans lied about voter fraud, Kansas' secretary of state was a voice of sanity"

### Defeated Conspiracy Challenger Mike Brown (2022 Primary)

- **Mike Brown** -- former Johnson County Commissioner, waged a campaign anchored to Trump's baseless 2020 fraud claims
- Brown accused Schwab of falling short as the state's top elections officer
- Brown embraced election conspiracy theories and promised to eliminate ballot drop boxes
- Brown had backing from conservative organizations and state lawmakers

### Confronted USPS Postmaster General DeJoy (2024)

**Background:** An estimated 1,000 Kansas primary election ballots were either properly postmarked but received after the three-day grace period, or lacked a required postmark altogether.

**Schwab's Response:**
- Sent a formal letter to Postmaster General Louis DeJoy on September 9, 2024
- Called the situation "unacceptable"
- Demanded three things from USPS:
  1. Explanation of USPS policy for ensuring every ballot is postmarked
  2. How USPS would ensure postmarking in the November general election
  3. How USPS would ensure timely delivery of every ballot placed in its custody before election day

**Outcome:**
- Schwab held a one-on-one call with DeJoy
- DeJoy acknowledged errors in processing Kansas primary ballots
- DeJoy reassured new measures would prevent similar issues
- However, Schwab later expressed being "disappointed" by USPS follow-through
- Source: [Kansas Secretary of State press release, October 10, 2024](https://sos.ks.gov/media/press-releases/2024/10-10-24-schwab-satisfied-with-one-on-one-call-with-usps-postmaster-general.html)

### Open Records Act Violation -- FULL DETAILS

**Case:** Loud Light & Hammet v. Schwab (2020)
**Filed by:** ACLU of Kansas on behalf of nonprofit Loud Light and its founder Davis Hammet

**What Happened:**
1. Davis Hammet, a voting rights advocate, requested provisional ballot data from Schwab's office
2. A district court ruled the records were public and ordered Schwab to release them
3. In August 2020 -- five months after the court's ruling -- Schwab asked the private company that stores the records to **shut off a software feature** that gave his office easy access to provisional ballot data
4. The company complied and turned off the function
5. When Hammet requested updated information, Schwab's office said it could no longer produce the data centrally -- unless Hammet agreed to pay **more than $500** to have a software expert spend three hours accessing it through a more complicated process

**Court Ruling (July 22, 2022):**
- Kansas Court of Appeals, three-judge panel, **unanimous** ruling
- **Judge Stephen Hill** wrote: "That action -- choosing to conceal rather than reveal public records -- violates KORA" (Kansas Open Records Act)
- ACLU described it as a "victory for government transparency and democracy"
- Davis Hammet stated it had been "mindboggling how aggressively" Schwab fought to withhold the records

**Post-Ruling Defiance:**
- Despite the appeals court ruling, Schwab's office **refused to provide provisional ballot records** as late as August 2022
- Kansas Reflector headline: "Kansas secretary of state refuses to provide provisional ballot records despite court ruling"

## 8. THE CALEB TRAGEDY

### The Incident: August 7, 2016

**What Happened:**

On Sunday, August 7, 2016, the Schwab family visited Schlitterbahn Waterpark in Kansas City, Kansas. The park was offering **free admission to Kansas elected officials and their families** that day. Scott was a sitting state representative.

Ten-year-old Caleb Schwab and his 12-year-old brother Nathan headed straight for **Verruckt** ("Insane" in German) -- the world's tallest water slide at 168 feet 7 inches, which had opened July 10, 2014. Caleb climbed the 264 steps to the top.

As the raft descended and went up Verruckt's second hill, it **became airborne**, launching Caleb into the air. He **collided with part of a metal support structure and netting** that spanned the slide. **Caleb was decapitated.** Two unrelated women seated behind him on the raft suffered facial injuries but survived.

Scott Schwab had given his standard reminder to Nathan and Caleb before they left: **"Brothers stick together."**

### The Funeral: August 12, 2016

**Location:** LifeMission Church, Olathe, Kansas
**Visitation:** Thursday, August 11, 6-8 PM at LifeMission Church
**Service:** Friday, August 12, afternoon

**Pastor Clint Sprague** (Lead Pastor, LifeMission Church) served as the family's spokesman from the moment the tragedy unfolded:
- "This has been devastating, it has been devastating at every level. You know you leave church you go to a waterpark to be with family and to enjoy and then you hit tragedy."
- "Caleb was an incredible young man. He's going to be missed for his energy, for his life, for his smile, for the way he lit up a room."

**Scott Schwab spoke at the funeral**, thanking many people who helped the family:
- Told a story: "Caleb was five at the time and he just turned around to me and said, 'Dad, I'm so sorry that happened to you.' And I just... whoa... I was the kid who scraped the knee at that moment and he held me."
- To Caleb's friends: "Thank you for helping shape his character and building memories that we will cherish forever. You guys are awesome."
- About his four sons: "When my kids were born, Nathan was my pleasure, Caleb was my joy, Alex was my delight and Isaac was my treasure."

**Pastor Rick Schwab** (Scott's brother) also spoke at the service, telling stories about Caleb and at times making the crowd laugh.

## 9. CANCER DIAGNOSIS

### Announcement: March 21, 2025

Scott Schwab announced on social media that he had been diagnosed with lung cancer.

**Key Details:**
- During a **routine cancer screening in December 2024**, doctors found a cancerous nodule in one of his lungs
- The nodule had **not spread or changed** since discovery
- Diagnosed as **Stage 1** -- caught very early
- Surgery was scheduled for the following week to remove the nodule
- Doctors confirmed **no further treatment would be required post-surgery**

**Schwab's Statement:** "I will make a full recovery."

He stated he would take some time to recover but remained committed to:
1. Serving as Secretary of State
2. Continuing his run for governor

### Post-Surgery Status

Schwab continued his campaign activities through 2025 and into 2026. At the January 30, 2026 GOP debate in Wichita, it was noted he **"struggled with a health issue that weakened his speaking voice"** -- though it is unclear if this was related to the lung surgery or a separate issue.

No public reports have indicated any recurrence or complications. His continued active campaigning through 2025-2026 suggests successful recovery.

## 15. CONTROVERSIES & LEGAL CHALLENGES

### 1. Kansas Open Records Act Violation (2020-2022) -- MAJOR

**Case:** Loud Light & Hammet v. Schwab
**Filed by:** ACLU of Kansas
**Ruling:** Kansas Court of Appeals, July 22, 2022 -- **Schwab violated KORA**
**Details:** See Section 5 for full account.
**Significance:** This is Schwab's most significant legal controversy. He directed a private company to disable software that gave his office access to provisional ballot data, effectively making public records inaccessible. He then continued to resist providing the records even after the court ruling.

### 2. League of Women Voters v. Schwab (2021-2024) -- SIGNIFICANT

**Plaintiffs:** League of Women Voters of Kansas, Loud Light, Kansas Appleseed Center for Law and Justice, Topeka Independent Living Resource Center
**Challenge:** Two new voting laws enacted in 2021 (HB 2183 and HB 2332) that:
- Made it a crime to "give the appearance of being an election official"
- Banned mailing advance ballot applications from out of state
- Tightened signature match requirements
- Forbade assisting in the collection of ten or more advance ballots

**Plaintiffs' argument:** The laws criminalize their voter registration and education activities
**Court rulings:**
- **May 2024:** Kansas Supreme Court upheld dismissal of claims against the ballot-collection restriction
- Supreme Court **remanded** the signature verification claims back to trial court
- Case was partially won, partially lost for both sides

### 3. DNC Lawsuit (2020) -- DISMISSED

**What happened:** Democratic National Committee chairman Tom Perez called Schwab "a carbon copy of his controversial predecessor" (Kris Kobach) and accused him of actions "designed to deter voting"
**Outcome:** A district court judge **dismissed** the Democrats' lawsuit to force Schwab to implement voting reform provisions for the 2020 elections

### 4. "Career Politician" Criticism

- Philip Sarnecki and others have criticized Schwab at debates for being a career politician
- Schwab has been in elected office or government positions almost continuously since 2003
- His response: "They call me a career politician, but no one's insulting my integrity"

### 5. Health Concerns

- The lung cancer diagnosis and visible health issues at the January 2026 debate (weakened voice) may become campaign vulnerabilities, though opponents have not publicly attacked him on health

### Assessment

Schwab's most significant vulnerability is the Open Records Act violation -- it's a court-documented finding that he actively worked to conceal public records, which cuts against the "transparency" image any Secretary of State should maintain. The League of Women Voters case adds to a pattern of resistance to voting access advocacy groups. However, neither controversy appears to have significantly damaged his standing with Republican primary voters.

## INVESTIGATOR'S ASSESSMENT

### Character Profile

Scott Schwab is a man whose life divides into two chapters: before August 7, 2016, and after. The death of his son Caleb on the Verruckt waterslide is the defining event of his personal life and has deeply shaped his public persona. His faith, which he expresses openly and with apparent genuineness, is the framework through which he has processed that tragedy.

Politically, Schwab is a mainstream conservative Republican with institutional experience -- 12+ years in the Kansas House, two terms as Secretary of State, NASS President. He is not a bomb-thrower or culture warrior. His defining political moment was standing up against election conspiracy theories in his own party after 2020, which he did clearly and directly ("I have seen no evidence of that").

The Open Records Act violation is his most significant mark against his record. Directing a vendor to disable access to public records, then claiming the records were too expensive to produce, is the kind of calculated obstruction that undermines public trust. His continued resistance even after the court ruling compounds this.

His 2026 campaign rests on property tax reform as the central issue, supported by his election integrity record and personal story. The self-funded nature of his campaign (over $1M personal loan, likely sourced from the Schlitterbahn settlement) gives him financial independence but may invite scrutiny.

### Strengths for Elect Righteous Analysis

- Authentic, documented Christian faith
- Deep personal suffering that has been publicly processed through faith
- Active church member (LifeMission Church, Olathe)
- Pro-life with personhood stance
- Pro-Second Amendment
- Strong on election integrity (implemented voter ID)
- Stood against conspiracy theories when it was politically costly

### Weaknesses for Elect Righteous Analysis

- Open Records Act violation -- documented willingness to conceal public information
- Accused by ACLU and voting rights groups of voter suppression
- DNC compared him to predecessor Kris Kobach (though lawsuit was dismissed)
- Career politician (government since 2003)
- Health concerns (lung cancer, weakened voice)
