### The 2012 Brownback Tax Vote

**The Vote:** In 2012, Schmidt voted with Republican colleagues for a bill slashing state income taxes, signed into law by Gov. Sam Brownback. The "Kansas experiment" was based on the theory that lowering the tax burden on wealthy individuals would unleash trickle-down economic forces beneficial to all.

**What Happened:** Revenue to the state treasury cratered over the subsequent five years. Lawmakers tried to fill the shortfall by raising the state sales tax and cutting budgets for core state services, including education and transportation. The tax cuts were eventually repealed in 2017.

**Her Regret (VERBATIM):**

> "It is a vote I regret. [...] The GOP strategy in the Senate was to place the tax bill in the hands of a House-Senate negotiating committee so a more balanced reform plan could be drafted. Instead of revisions, the House concurred with the Senate and Brownback signed the bill into law."

> "We know how that all turned out. I sincerely regret that I trusted the process to move that along, but I will tell you that I learned a big lesson that day. You can't always trust the process."
> -- Kansas Reflector podcast, July 2025

### Abortion Votes -- THE KEY CONTROVERSY

**The Facts:**

Vicki Schmidt was the **ONLY Republican** in the Kansas Senate to vote against **BOTH** of the following anti-abortion bills:

1. **HB 2035 (2011)** -- A bill blocking taxpayer-funded abortions. Schmidt voted against it. Final action vote: March 23, 2011.

2. **SB 95 (2015)** -- The "Unborn Child Protection from Dismemberment Abortion Act," banning dilation and evacuation (D&E) procedures, the most common method for second-trimester abortions. Kansas was the first state in the nation to pass such a ban. The Senate vote was **31-9**. Schmidt was the lone Republican "no" vote. Vote date: February 20, 2015.

**Kansans for Life noted that her votes on these bills mirrored those of then-Senator Laura Kelly**, the current Democratic governor.

She was the only Republican in the Kansas Senate to oppose both a bill blocking taxpayer-funded abortions and a bill limiting live dismemberment abortions -- positions that mirrored those of then-Senator Laura Kelly, with whom she closely aligned."

> "Kansans for Life PAC will not allow Commissioner Schmidt to hide from her record, nor should primary voters let her slip through what could be a crowded field."

**Significance:** This is arguably the single most damaging opposition statement against Schmidt in the primary. In a Kansas Republican primary, having KFL PAC actively working against you is a significant headwind.

### The 2012 Primary Survival

In 2012, during the Brownback "purge" of moderate Republicans, Koch Industries and the Kansas Chamber of Commerce orchestrated large donations to recruit conservative challengers against moderate Republican legislators. Schmidt was targeted.

**She survived -- barely.** In a primary with over 11,000 votes cast, she won by only 160 votes. She was one of the very few moderate Republicans to survive the 2012 purge. Many others, including Jean Schodorf (a comparable moderate Republican woman), were defeated.

The bill also created the Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) Licensure Act. Coverage was added to large group plans, small grandfathered plans, and individual grandfathered plans.

Schmidt helped provide seed money for the University of Kansas Cancer Center during its pursuit of NCI (National Cancer Institute) designation. The center eventually achieved NCI comprehensive designation in 2022, becoming one of fewer than 60 such centers in the United States.

Schmidt is attempting to position herself as a pragmatic moderate on abortion in a Kansas context where:

## 10. THE BREAST CANCER SCREENING BILL (SB 409)

### The Bill

**Senate Bill 409 (2026 Session)**

**What it does:** Requires any insurance plan in Kansas that already covers diagnostic and supplemental breast imaging to eliminate out-of-pocket costs for:
- Diagnostic mammograms (follow-up tests after routine mammograms)
- MRIs
- Ultrasounds
- Any imaging prescribed after inconclusive initial mammogram results

This affects nearly half of all women over 40 who have dense breast tissue, which often requires additional imaging beyond a standard mammogram.

### The Hearing

SB 409 was heard February 2, 2026, in the Senate Committee on Financial Institutions and Insurance. Commissioner Schmidt provided testimony blending her personal breast cancer experience with policy advocacy. The hearing preceded the Susan G. Komen Day of Action on the Capitol (February 4, 2026).

### How It Was Killed

The bill missed key legislative deadlines and is considered dead for the 2026 session. Two competing explanations:

**Democratic Explanation (and most media reporting):**
The bill was killed because Insurance Commissioner Vicki Schmidt championed it, and Schmidt is running against **Senate President Ty Masterson** in the Republican gubernatorial primary. Masterson controls the legislative calendar and committee assignments. Democrats and some reporters characterized this as pure political retaliation.

**Republican Leadership Explanation:**
A spokesman for Masterson said politics had nothing to do with the bill's failure. Republican leadership attributed the stall to "pushback from the business community" over mandated coverage costs.

### Significance

This is a politically toxic situation for Masterson and potentially advantageous for Schmidt:
- It allows Schmidt to position herself as a champion of women's health against her own party's leadership
- It gives her a ready-made general election narrative about putting people over politics
- The optics of Republican men killing a breast cancer screening bill championed by a breast cancer survivor are terrible for the GOP brand

## 11. DEBATE PERFORMANCE & PAY-TO-PLAY CONTROVERSY

### The January 30, 2026 GOP Debate

**Schmidt did NOT participate.** She boycotted the debate over the Kansas Republican Party's requirement that candidates pay $10,000 to participate.

### Her Objection (Verbatim)

In a letter to GOP officials, Schmidt stated:

> "Under this agreement, the party picks winners and losers."

> "President Trump has brought new voters to the party and we need to be encouraging good folks to run for office as Republicans, not creating road blocks and challenges that discourage them."

She argued the Republican Party should remain neutral during the primary campaign and that every GOP candidate should have equal opportunities to share their views at party-sanctioned events.

### The Debate Itself

Six Republican candidates participated without Schmidt: Jeff Colyer, Scott Schwab, Ty Masterson, Doug Billings, Joy Eakins, and Charlotte O'Hara (and/or Stacy Rogers). The Kansas Reflector described it as "Six Kansas GOP candidates for governor toss haymakers in first debate, find unity on key issues." Candidates found consensus on abortion, marijuana opposition, judicial bias concerns, and praise for President Trump.

### Assessment

Schmidt's boycott was a calculated gamble:
- **Upside:** She avoids being attacked on her abortion record in a hostile environment, maintains her "above the fray" positioning, and scores points with voters skeptical of party establishment
- **Downside:** She misses a chance to introduce herself to primary voters, and opponents can frame her as afraid to face scrutiny
- **Result:** The debate produced no breakout moments for any candidate, suggesting Schmidt lost little by skipping it

### The Case AGAINST Schmidt's Electability

1. **Primary Vulnerability:** She must first survive a Republican primary where her abortion votes are a liability. KFL PAC's active opposition could consolidate anti-Schmidt votes.

2. **Brownback Tax Vote:** The DGA (Democratic Governors Association) has already flagged this: "Schmidt brings to this race a disastrous and disqualifying record of voting to enact Sam Brownback's failed tax experiment."

3. **No Formal Polling:** No head-to-head general election polls between Schmidt and any Democratic candidate were found in this investigation.

### The "Bob Dole Republican" Analysis

The Mittelpolitan Substack and Lawrence Journal-World published analyses comparing Schmidt to former senator Jean Schodorf, framing both as "Bob Dole Republicans" -- moderates who "think in terms of moderation" or, as Schmidt puts it, "use common sense."

The key difference: Schodorf was defeated in the 2012 Brownback purge. Schmidt survived -- barely (by 160 votes). This survival instinct and ability to thread the needle between moderate positions and Republican primary voters is central to her political identity.

Schmidt's path mirrors a long Kansas tradition of moderate Republican women who emphasize pragmatism over ideology. Whether that tradition can survive in a Trump-era Republican primary is the central question of her candidacy.

## 16. OTHER NOTABLE ACTIONS

### Kansas Young Republicans Scandal (October 2025)

Schmidt was among the first Kansas Republicans to publicly condemn Alex Dwyer (chair) and William Hendrix (vice chair) of the Kansas Young Republicans after Politico exposed racist, violent messages in an encrypted Telegram chat. Hendrix had used racial slurs and praised anti-LGBTQ+ positions.

Schmidt's tweet calling for their resignations was forceful and immediate. The Kansas Young Republicans chapter was subsequently shut down.

### Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM) Reform

As Insurance Commissioner, Schmidt has called for PBM reform -- an issue directly tied to her pharmacy background. PBMs are intermediaries that manage prescription drug benefits and have been criticized for opaque pricing practices.
