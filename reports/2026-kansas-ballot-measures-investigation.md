# MEASURE 1: KANSAS ELECTIONS FOR SUPREME COURT JUSTICES AMENDMENT
**Election Date: August 4, 2026 (Primary Ballot)**
**Type: Legislatively Referred Constitutional Amendment (SCR 1611)**

---

## THE AMENDMENT -- WHAT IT ACTUALLY SAYS

The amendment modifies Sections 5, 8, and 15 of Article 3 of the Kansas Constitution. The ballot language reads:

> **"The citizens of Kansas who are qualified electors shall elect the justices of the supreme court."**

A YES vote means:
- Kansas Supreme Court justices would be directly elected by voters at staggered elections
- The Kansas Supreme Court Nominating Commission (whose membership consists of a majority of lawyers) would be abolished
- Justices would serve 6-year terms
- Positions 1, 2, 3 elected in November 2028; Positions 4, 5 elected in November 2030; Positions 6, 7 elected in November 2032

A NO vote means:
- The current merit-based system continues
- The Kansas Supreme Court Nominating Commission continues providing the governor a list of three individuals to choose from for vacancies

**Critical detail the amendment does NOT specify:** Whether elections would be partisan or nonpartisan, how campaigns would be financed, or what the nomination process would look like. Those details would be left to the Kansas Legislature to decide -- a legislature that is overwhelmingly Republican.

As retired Justice Carol Beier stated: **"It is exceedingly vague. It will be decided by the Legislature. Do you trust them?"**

---

## HOW THE CURRENT SYSTEM WORKS (MERIT SELECTION)

### The Kansas Supreme Court Nominating Commission
- **9 members**: One lawyer and one non-lawyer from each of the state's four congressional districts, plus one additional lawyer serving as chair
- **Lawyer members** elected by their peers (active Kansas attorneys)
- **Non-lawyer members** appointed by the governor
- When a vacancy opens, the commission reviews applications, conducts public interviews, and submits **three qualified names** to the governor
- The governor must select from those three names within 60 days
- Justices then face **retention elections** every 6 years (voters decide yes/no on keeping them)

### Candidate Requirements
- Must be a licensed attorney in Kansas
- Over age 30
- At least 10 years active as a lawyer, judge, or law professor

### Why This System Exists: The "Triple Play" Scandal of 1956-57

The merit selection system was born from one of Kansas's most infamous political scandals:

1. **December 31, 1956**: Kansas Supreme Court Chief Justice William Smith (a longtime Republican party stalwart) resigned
2. **January 3, 1957**: Governor Fred Hall -- who had just lost reelection -- resigned, just 11 days before his term ended
3. **Immediately**: Lieutenant Governor John McCuish (who was literally in a hospital in Newton and had to be brought back to Topeka by a Highway Patrolman) was automatically elevated to Governor and, in his sole official act, appointed Hall to the Supreme Court

The backlash was immediate and severe. Kansans were outraged at this backroom dealing. In **1958**, voters approved a constitutional amendment creating the nonpartisan nominating commission system that has been in place for **67 years**.

Kansas is the **only state** in the country with a "bar-controlled" merit-selection system -- a distinction supporters view as a strength and critics view as elitism.

## THE REAL MOTIVATION: ABORTION AND SCHOOL FUNDING

This is the part that requires the most honest analysis.

### The 2019 Abortion Ruling
In April 2019, the Kansas Supreme Court ruled 6-1 in *Hodes & Nauser v. Schmidt* that the Kansas Constitution's Bill of Rights protects a woman's right to "personal autonomy," which includes whether to continue or end a pregnancy. This made Kansas the first state to establish abortion rights through its state constitution via court ruling.

### The 2022 Abortion Amendment (The Precedent)
In August 2022, Kansas voters **overwhelmingly rejected** (59% to 41%) a constitutional amendment that would have removed abortion protections. This was the first post-*Dobbs* statewide abortion vote in America and sent shockwaves through national politics. Turnout was nearly **50%** for a primary -- almost double normal primary turnout.

### The Gannon School Funding Cases
The Kansas Supreme Court spent over a decade (2010-2024) monitoring whether the state Legislature adequately funded public schools. The court repeatedly ordered the Legislature to increase school funding. Conservative lawmakers were furious.

### Connecting the Dots
Kansas Reflector explicitly headlined their coverage: **"Kansas Senate adopts plan to elect Supreme Court justices, a step toward overturning abortion rights."**

The reporting reveals that Kansas Republicans returned from the 2024 Republican National Convention dismayed by the party's softened stance on abortion. House Majority Leader Chris Croft acknowledged that the ripple effect of Kansas voters supporting abortion rights in 2022 was "part of the problem."

**Bottom line**: The amendment's primary backers are conservative legislators and organizations who are unhappy with the court's rulings on abortion rights and school funding. Electing justices on partisan ballots in a Republican-dominated state would be the mechanism to eventually install a court majority willing to reverse those rulings.

## BALLOT TIMING: THE AUGUST PRIMARY STRATEGY

### Why August Matters
The amendment is on the **August 4, 2026 primary ballot** -- not the November general election. This matters enormously:

- **Primary turnout is far lower** than general election turnout (typically 20-25% vs. 50-60%)
- **Kansas primaries are closed**: Only voters registered with a party can vote in that party's primary. Unaffiliated voters receive a ballot with ONLY the amendment question.
- **Republicans typically dominate primary turnout** because they run more contested primary races
- **Democratic-leaning voters** have less reason to show up for primaries, especially in safe blue districts

### The 2022 Precedent
The 2022 abortion amendment was ALSO placed on an August primary ballot -- widely seen as a strategy to suppress turnout from the opposition. It backfired spectacularly: turnout surged to nearly 50%, driven by pro-choice voters, and the amendment was crushed.

The question for 2026: **Will the judicial selection amendment generate the same counter-mobilization?** KCUR and other outlets have raised serious concerns about whether enough voters will understand the stakes and show up.

## NATIONAL CONTEXT: WHAT HAPPENS WHEN STATES ELECT JUDGES

### States With Partisan Supreme Court Elections
Eight states currently use partisan elections for their supreme courts. The evidence from those states is not encouraging for advocates of judicial independence:

**Wisconsin (2023)**: The most expensive judicial election in U.S. history -- **over $100 million spent**. The race became a proxy war over abortion rights and redistricting, with national money flooding the state.

**North Carolina (2022)**: After partisan elections flipped the court's majority to conservative, the new majority took the extraordinary step of **rehearing and reversing** several predecessor decisions on redistricting and voting rights -- decisions that had been issued just months earlier.

**Pennsylvania**: Supreme Court races have become multi-million-dollar partisan battles, with campaign spending increasing dramatically each cycle.

### Academic Research
- Research published in the *Journal of Public Economics* found that **judges selected by nonpartisan processes produce higher-quality work** than judges selected by partisan elections
- The Brennan Center for Justice documented that **campaign spending in state supreme court elections** has increased dramatically, with special interests playing an outsized role
- Duke University's *Judicature* journal found that **partisanship has increased** across all types of judicial elections from 1981-2020

# MEASURE 2: KANSAS CITIZENSHIP VOTING REQUIREMENT AMENDMENT
**Election Date: November 3, 2026 (General Election)**
**Type: Legislatively Referred Constitutional Amendment (HCR 5004)**

---

## THE AMENDMENT -- WHAT IT ACTUALLY SAYS

HCR 5004 amends Section 1 of Article 5 of the Kansas Constitution.

**Current language**: "Every citizen of the United States who has attained the age of eighteen years and who resides in the voting area in which he or she seeks to vote shall be deemed a qualified elector."

**Proposed new language**: "**No person shall be deemed a qualified elector unless such person**: (1) Is a citizen of the United States..."

The ballot question reads:
> "This amendment would clarify only a person who is a citizen of the United States is eligible to vote in this state. A vote for this proposition would clarify that only a person who is a citizen of the United States is eligible to vote in this state. A vote against this proposition would make no change to the Constitution of the State of Kansas, and the language concerning voter qualifications would remain the same."

### The Critical Distinction
The amendment changes the constitutional framing from **inclusionary** ("Every citizen... shall be deemed qualified") to **exclusionary** ("No person shall be deemed qualified unless..."). This is the shift from "every" to "only" -- the same linguistic change adopted by eight other states in 2024.

---

## WHAT THIS ACTUALLY CHANGES (LEGALLY)

**The honest answer: Almost nothing -- immediately.**

It is **already illegal** for non-citizens to vote in Kansas. Under both existing Kansas state law and federal law, only U.S. citizens may vote in state and federal elections. This has been the law for as long as Kansas has existed as a state.

The amendment does not create a new prohibition. It changes the *framing* of the existing constitutional language.

However, opponents argue the real impact is in what it **enables down the road** -- specifically, it could provide constitutional ammunition for future legislation imposing proof-of-citizenship requirements for voter registration.

---

## LEGISLATIVE VOTES

- **House**: Passed 90-28 on February 5, 2025 (7 not voting/absent)
- **Senate**: Passed 37-3 on March 25, 2025
- **Bipartisan support**: All voting Republicans AND nine Democrats voted YES in the House. All "No" votes were Democrats. Senate opposition was minimal (only 3 nays).

This received far more bipartisan support than the judicial selection amendment.

## IS THIS A SOLUTION IN SEARCH OF A PROBLEM?

### The Evidence on Non-Citizen Voting in Kansas

**Short answer: Yes.** The evidence overwhelmingly shows non-citizen voting is essentially nonexistent in Kansas.

### The Fish v. Kobach Case (2018)
This is the definitive case on this issue in Kansas. When Kris Kobach was Secretary of State, he championed the Kansas SAFE Act (2011), which required documentary proof of citizenship (birth certificate or passport) to register to vote.

The ACLU sued. At trial, the court found:
- Since 1999, Kansas officials had confirmed only **127 cases** of noncitizens who had either registered or attempted to register
- Only **43** successfully registered
- Only **11** actually voted (over a period of nearly 20 years)
- Meanwhile, the law **blocked 31,089 eligible American citizens** from registering

Federal Judge Julie Robinson's conclusion:
> **"There is no iceberg; only an icicle, largely created by confusion and administrative error."**

The judge found **no credible evidence** that a substantial number of noncitizens registered to vote. Kobach's proof-of-citizenship law was struck down as violating the National Voter Registration Act and the U.S. Constitution. The 10th Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the ruling.

### The Ratio
- **39 noncitizens** found on Kansas voter rolls (many due to administrative error)
- **35,000+ eligible citizens** blocked from registering
- That is a ratio of roughly **1 noncitizen for every 897 blocked citizens**

## IMPLICATIONS: WHAT COULD HAPPEN NEXT

The ACLU's concern is not about the amendment itself but about what it **enables**:

1. **Proof-of-citizenship revival**: The exclusionary language could provide constitutional justification for Kansas to re-enact a proof-of-citizenship requirement for voter registration -- the same type of law struck down in Fish v. Kobach
2. **Additional ID barriers**: Could be used to justify requiring additional documentation beyond what is currently needed
3. **Chilling effect**: The framing could discourage naturalized citizens from voting out of confusion or fear
