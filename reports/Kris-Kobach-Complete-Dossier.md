# SECTION 2: SECRETARY OF STATE RECORD (2011-2019)

## The SAFE Act and Documentary Proof of Citizenship (DPOC)

In 2011, Kobach persuaded the Kansas Legislature to pass the Kansas Secure and Fair Elections (SAFE) Act, his signature legislation. It required all new voter registrants to present documentary proof of U.S. citizenship (birth certificate, passport, or naturalization papers) before being allowed to register. The law took effect in 2013.

**Impact**: More than **35,000 eligible Kansas voters** were blocked from registering under this law.

**The Trial -- Fish v. Kobach (2018)**:
- Trial hearings ran March 6-19, 2018, before Chief U.S. District Judge Julie A. Robinson
- Kobach personally served as lead attorney defending the law (despite being Secretary of State, not a practicing litigator)
- Judge Robinson **struck down the DPOC law as unconstitutional** on June 18, 2018, finding it violated the National Voter Registration Act and the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment
- Robinson's 118-page opinion was a point-by-point dismantling of Kobach's arguments
- She found that Kobach could demonstrate only **39 cases of noncitizen voter registration** in Kansas over a nearly 20-year period out of 1.8 million registered voters
- She noted Kobach's "evidence of the iceberg" theory of widespread fraud was not supported by any credible evidence

## Interstate Crosscheck Voter Purge System

**What it was**: The Interstate Voter Registration Crosscheck Program (IVRC), administered by the Kansas Secretary of State's office under Kobach, aggregated voter registration records from participating states to identify voters who appeared to be registered in more than one state.

**Scale of voter removals**: An estimated **1.1 million voters had their registrations purged** across 30 states before the 2016 election based on Crosscheck data.

**Accuracy problems**:
- The system flagged **7.2 million voters** total
- **Less than one in a million** of those flagged had actually voted twice
- For every double vote prevented, Crosscheck impeded approximately **300 legitimate registrations**
- The system disproportionately impacted minority voters: **1 in 6 Latino voters, 1 in 7 Asian-American voters, and 1 in 9 Black voters** in participating states were flagged
- Common names (e.g., "James Brown," "Maria Garcia") triggered false matches at extremely high rates because the system matched primarily on first name, last name, and date of birth without verifying Social Security numbers

**Collapse of the program**:
- Ten states withdrew from Crosscheck before it was suspended
- In **December 2019**, the program was **suspended indefinitely** as part of an ACLU of Kansas lawsuit settlement
- Security failures were also cited -- voter data was transmitted between states via unencrypted methods

7. **ESG/Vanguard-BlackRock-State Street Lawsuit**: Joined Texas-led 13-state coalition suing major asset managers over alleged ESG collusion. **Settlement with Vanguard** (February 2026): Vanguard agreed to strict passivity commitments and **$29.5 million payment** to participating states.

### State-Level Litigation

8. **Kansas v. Harper -- Transgender ID/Driver's License Case (July 2023)**: Filed against Kansas Department of Revenue to prohibit transgender people from changing gender markers on driver's licenses. 9. **SB 244 -- Transgender Bathroom/ID Law (2026)**: After losing in courts, Kobach went to the legislature. Kansas Supreme Court **dismissed** Kelly's petition on narrow procedural grounds (March 27, 2026). Both sides declared victory. The court's opinion stated the governor may litigate to protect executive interests, and the AG may not enlarge his authority to bar that exercise.

12. **Snapchat Lawsuit**: Sued Snap Inc. alleging deceptive and unconscionable acts harming consumers, particularly teenagers.

13. **THC Smoke Shop Raids (2025-2026)**: Kobach's office directed raids on smoke shops in October 2025. Before and during his time as Secretary of State, Kobach maintained a lucrative side practice as an immigration enforcement attorney, working primarily for the Immigration Reform Law Institute (IRLI, the legal arm of the Federation for American Immigration Reform/FAIR) and for municipalities seeking to pass anti-immigration ordinances.

# SECTION 14: OVERALL ASSESSMENT

## The Kobach Paradox

Kris Kobach presents a genuine paradox. He holds elite academic credentials from three of the world's most prestigious universities (Harvard, Oxford, Yale). He has been a law professor, a White House Fellow, and counsel to the U.S. Attorney General. On paper, he should be one of the most formidable lawyers in state government.

And yet, the record shows a consistent pattern of **overreach followed by courtroom failure**:
- He drafted immigration ordinances that were struck down across the country, leaving cities bankrupt
- He passed a voter proof-of-citizenship law that was struck down, costing Kansas $4+ million
- He was held in contempt of court and ordered to take remedial law classes
- He led a presidential commission that found nothing and was disbanded
- He administered a voter crosscheck system that purged 1.1 million legitimate voters with a false positive rate exceeding 99.9999%
- He lost a governor's race in a deep-red state

As AG, his record is more mixed. He has secured genuine consumer protection settlements, built a functional fentanyl enforcement program, and won significant federal injunctions against Biden administration policies. But he has also continued the pattern of overreach -- losing the gender marker case in court, then going to the legislature; directing smoke shop raids now facing Fourth Amendment challenges; and engaging in an unprecedented power struggle with the Governor.

## The 2026 Race

Kobach won by only 1.6 points in 2022 in a state where Republicans typically win statewide by 10+ points. The 2022 abortion referendum revealed a Kansas electorate far more moderate than its Republican establishment. Chris Mann is a credible challenger with a compelling personal story (former cop hit by a drunk driver, national MADD chairman).

Kobach's advantages: incumbency, $502,626 cash on hand, Trump alignment in a red state, tangible AG accomplishments to point to (fentanyl program, federal litigation wins).

Kobach's vulnerabilities: razor-thin 2022 margin, extensive record of courtroom losses and judicial sanctions, the Morgan & Morgan firing, ongoing THC raid lawsuit, the cost of his SOS-era litigation to taxpayers, and the fact that his most prominent work as AG (anti-Biden lawsuits, anti-transgender litigation) may not resonate with the moderate suburban voters who determine close Kansas races.
