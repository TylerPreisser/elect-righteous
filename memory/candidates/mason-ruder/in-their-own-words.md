# Mason Ruder - In Their Own Words

Mason Ruder's public messaging is not built around a visible campaign-social apparatus. Through May 11, 2026, the clearest public record comes from Hays City Commission meetings, Hays Post reporting, a 2023 candidate forum, Kansas legislative testimony, LinkedIn public-preview activity, and his Hays Catholic Schools role.

The dominant theme is local growth tied to practical infrastructure. In his 2023 re-election comments, Ruder said city decisions affect residents "almost immediately" and closed with: "Let's keep growing Hays. We have a lot of work to do." At the 2023 candidate forum, he pointed to partnerships with business leaders, Grow Hays, and community institutions to grow the workforce and develop housing, while also making R9 water completion a priority. Sources: [Hays Post, Nov. 8, 2023](https://hayspost.com/posts/5c04aec5-5ab3-413c-8844-d90e91aa0d31), [Hays Post, Oct. 18, 2023](https://hayspost.com/posts/686c41a0-31e3-415a-8fdf-3df810e7f5be).

Water security is his most consistent policy lane. Ruder has described the R9 Ranch project as an everyday governing priority and later as a "multi-generational battle." In 2026, during the HB 2433 fight over county versus state water-transfer authority, he argued that Edwards County's actions forced Hays and Russell into a second regulatory process controlled by a county trying to stop the project. Sources: [Hays Post, Jan. 16, 2023](https://hayspost.com/posts/a356c3c3-47d3-49eb-98d5-fe030ffd2b8e), [Hays Post, Aug. 29, 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/72cef8d3-79bf-4085-b070-bccbdc589f9d), [Hays Post/Kansas Reflector, Feb. 6, 2026](https://hayspost.com/posts/0b77976f-deca-4758-819a-f01bbc3f4745).

On development and taxes, Ruder's remarks are pro-growth but framed through fiscal mechanics. He backed incentives for an I-70 travel plaza after citing an estimated city return of almost 2,200%, and he argued that Kansas' vacant-land valuation structure makes it cheaper to hold land than build. He also floated marijuana tax revenue as a possible way to offset property taxes and help sales-tax-reliant communities. Sources: [Hays Post, Feb. 21, 2026](https://hayspost.com/posts/6988fcbc-170e-42d1-9dbd-cb28e017baba), [Hays Post, Dec. 20, 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/31a9d9f8-0883-480f-aada-22ac084f09b9).

In 2026, that growth message extended into affordable housing and workforce innovation. During consideration of IRB assistance for the 48-unit Pioneer Run project, Ruder said the developer's properties "look great," are well run, and provide an affordable place to live. Ahead of an FHSU AI Hackathon, he framed AI as a local choice: communities can shape it or let it happen to them, and the places that come out ahead will invest in people who want to build something worthwhile. Sources: [Tiger Media Network, Apr. 24, 2026](https://tigermedianet.com/?p=97002), [Hays Post/FHSU, Mar. 31, 2026](https://hayspost.com/posts/559f8a20-030b-4d18-a708-67bffc879730).

His infrastructure language is similarly practical. On the North Vine roundabout corridor, he called the project "an elegant solution to a complex problem" and emphasized safety, traffic flow, pedestrian access, and redevelopment potential. On airport service, he warned that replacing a proposed Essential Air Service cut locally would require a major mill levy increase. Sources: [Hays Post/KDOT, Aug. 10, 2022](https://hayspost.com/posts/b4422e87-ed3f-4d70-937d-b25e3a396187), [Hays Post, May 24, 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/2730c5cd-1026-4098-9e5f-ebf15c8fd949).

Ruder also presents himself through faith, family, and Hays institutional life. TMP-Marian's profile announcing him as Hays Catholic Schools advancement director quotes him saying the role would help his "community, my school, my family" and that his local Catholic relationships would be an asset. Later coverage of TMP-Marian's Auction for Christian Education has him emphasizing alumni and community generosity, continuity with previous generations, and the strength of Catholic education support in Ellis County. Sources: [TMP-Marian, Nov. 30, 2022](https://www.tmp-m.org/o/tmpm/article/923424), [Hays Post, May 5, 2024](https://hayspost.com/posts/46c0cb3c-bd1a-435f-9d5a-598963fe942a).

His civic style is framed around collaboration and institutional continuity. When he was elected mayor in January 2026, he thanked outgoing mayor Sandy Jacobs for bringing people together "not just to talk, but to actually move work forward through collaboration," and described practical, community-minded economic development as a strength for Hays. Source: [Tiger Media Network, Jan. 12, 2026](https://tigermedianet.com/?p=93385).

The visible social footprint is thin. A public LinkedIn preview for Mason Ruder, CPM, shows 151 followers and 131 connections and visible liked/amplified items around R9, Northwest Kansas economic development, Kansas SBDC, childcare, housing, and mental health/community support. Full LinkedIn activity is login-gated, and no reliable public campaign website, campaign Facebook page, X/Twitter, Instagram, or candidate-controlled YouTube channel surfaced in this pass.

Methodology: I reviewed public sources visible through May 11, 2026, including city pages and agenda materials, Hays Post and Hays Post archive coverage, Kansas Legislature HB 2433 testimony listings and reporting, TMP-Marian/Hays Catholic Schools pages, LinkedIn public search previews, and targeted searches for Facebook, Instagram, X/Twitter, campaign sites, and video. I did not fabricate inaccessible social content. Where platforms were login-walled, script-blocked, or not clearly attributable, I treated them as absences or previews rather than verified posts.

Disclaimer: This summary reflects public, candidate-attributed or candidate-amplified messaging found in accessible sources. It is not a complete archive of private social media, deleted posts, login-only activity, or every public meeting statement.
