# Nathan Leiker In Their Own Words

Nathan Leiker's public messaging is grounded in county operations and agriculture, not in a large campaign-social presence. The official [Ellis County Commission page](https://www.ellisco.net/120/County-Commission) lists him as the Third District commissioner, gives the public meeting schedule, and points residents to agendas, minutes, and the county's YouTube livestream. The county-controlled record is therefore one of the main ways his current public voice appears.

His 2022 campaign language presented him as a local farm-and-ranch operator with zoning, Extension, oil-field, and rural-fire experience. In his [campaign announcement](https://hayspost.com/posts/aedbf614-0ea1-4a7c-8932-156b271a4709), he credited his family and said he had worked in positions that took him throughout the county. He described himself as proud to be from Ellis County and ready to serve it.

The clearest platform document is his [Hays Post candidate Q&A](https://hayspost.com/posts/599cb8a3-fed5-4c96-bb58-7079dcedac17). There, Leiker argued for keeping the county sales tax and making it permanent before sunset, but tying those dollars to infrastructure and large capital improvements. He said county voters worry about where their money is going, and he connected transparency to visible evidence of roads, bridges, and public work actually getting done.

Infrastructure is the dominant through-line. After the 2022 canvass, he said he wanted increased focus on roads, bridges, and the infrastructure needed to grow the county, according to [Hays Post](https://hayspost.com/posts/6e260438-27db-4402-bf8e-e8df621996d4). In 2024, when Ellis County discussed rural road maintenance, he said the county had promoted subdivision growth without growing public works capacity to handle the added residents, as reported by [Hays Post](https://hayspost.com/posts/0ec5af1e-3acd-4f02-9842-bf61a57eae9a). In 2025, he suggested dividing the county into regions and using a project manager or team to prioritize right-of-way and road-maintenance issues, again in [Hays Post coverage](https://hayspost.com/posts/c7baa7e1-bdbb-4850-9c17-62b30660f9e0).

Leiker's development message is pro-growth but bounded by planning and infrastructure capacity. In the candidate Q&A, he said the county should recruit business by keeping taxes stable and showing it can maintain infrastructure. At an August 2024 joint city-county meeting, he called the Vineyard Road discussion a forward-looking project and said Hays was clearly developing north of I-70, according to [Hays Post](https://hayspost.com/posts/2584b551-eb90-4e5b-abbf-fd3ef6cab0bc). But he also opposed the Fairview Patch subdivision, saying in [May 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/4f685bbb-15b3-4649-acc2-fae5a0efb0bd) that the location worked against the comprehensive plan and in [July 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/fa81e374-6f44-4875-8984-b7869e10c11d) that it was getting far from localized subdivision patterns. In [March 2026](https://hayspost.com/posts/d4ad7eb6-4af1-419c-b0ec-e8786acdca3d), he suggested pausing new plats until subdivision regulations could be updated.

On budgets and taxes, he speaks as a fiscal conservative who still accepts spending when tied to services, infrastructure, and risk management. In [October 2024 budget coverage](https://hayspost.com/posts/d81583db-0414-44c6-8dac-7d4ad565ed13), he said commissioners try to limit their tax-dollar impact but warned against deferring bridge and road needs until they become failures or accidents. In [August 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/7c57b3c8-1314-4bd7-8501-f559ff7c176d), he said a proposed mill increase would give the county a soft landing if state or federal grants were pulled. In [December 2025](https://hayspost.com/posts/0dc3bf76-bd9f-4190-a0c7-231ca8172d18), he complained that counties keep losing tools from their toolbox and then get told they are not doing the job right.

His agriculture messaging predates county office and reinforces the same practical tone. In [Ingram's 2021 profile](https://ingrams.com/article/50-kansans-you-should-know-2021/), Leiker described L5 Farms as part of a fifth-generation farm in Ellis County and talked about direct producer-consumer relationships after COVID exposed the fragility of food-supply relationships. In a [2023 MyAnIML case study](https://myaniml.com/Kansas_Rancher_Installs_MyAnIML_to_Save.pdf), he framed cattle-health technology in terms of labor limits, earlier treatment, low-stress handling, and finding operational improvements through better monitoring.

Leiker's limited-government message appears most sharply in the candidate Q&A. He opposed expanding the county commission from three members to five, saying more government is not a good thing and that more managers at the top are not the answer. He also criticized a prior zoning denial after zoning-board approval and said government should have very little authority over what people do on private property apart from illegal actions.

The highest visible attention marker is electoral rather than social. Hays Post reported Leiker with 4,193 votes in the 2024 general election on election night, and the [Ellis County official results PDF](https://www.ellisco.net/DocumentCenter/View/5620/2024-Official-Results) later listed 4,272 votes and 45 write-ins. I did not find reliable public follower, like, share, comment, or view counts for a Leiker-controlled campaign or profile account.

The absences are important. I found no verified active campaign website, no reliable public campaign Facebook page, and no verified X/Twitter, Instagram, Threads, TikTok, or YouTube campaign/profile account. A LinkedIn URL appears in prior local notes, but it was not publicly fetchable in this environment and did not yield accessible post or audience metrics. County YouTube links are meeting records, not a Leiker campaign channel. The reviewed public record also did not surface Leiker-authored statements on abortion, LGBTQ issues, school curriculum, national immigration policy, election fraud, or federal partisan issues.

Methodology: I reviewed public search results and accessible pages for Nathan Leiker, Nathan D. Leiker, Ellis County Commissioner District 3, L5 Farms, Hays Post coverage, Ellis County commission pages, agendas, minutes, media links, public social platforms, LinkedIn, Facebook, X/Twitter, Instagram, Threads, TikTok, YouTube, agriculture profiles, ranch technology references, and local election results through May 11, 2026. I prioritized official/county pages, candidate-authored Q&A answers, attributed direct quotes, public meeting coverage, and profile-controlled or attributed agriculture/professional remarks.

Disclaimer: This is a public-source messaging summary, not a complete biography, endorsement, opposition file, or legal assessment. I did not reconstruct deleted, private, login-walled, script-blocked, or ambiguous social content, and I did not attribute accounts or posts to Leiker unless the public evidence supported doing so.
