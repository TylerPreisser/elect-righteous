# Kansas Elections for Supreme Court Justices Constitutional Amendment (August 2026)

## Key Dates
- **Election Date**: 2026-08-04 (on the PRIMARY ballot, NOT the general election)
- **Voter Registration Deadline**: 2026-07-14

## Position(s)
- Constitutional Amendment -- Yes/No vote
- This is NOT a candidate race. This is a ballot measure.
- Legislatively referred constitutional amendment (SCR 1611)

## Jurisdiction
- **Level**: State -- Constitutional Amendment
- **District/Ward**: Statewide
- **Eligible Voters**: All registered Kansas voters who vote in the August 4 primary

## What the Amendment Would Do
- **ABOLISH** the existing Kansas Supreme Court Nominating Commission (merit-based selection system in place since 1958)
- **REPLACE** it with direct partisan elections of Kansas Supreme Court justices
- Authorize the Kansas State Legislature to develop rules regarding the elections
- Justices' terms would remain 6 years
- Vacancies would be filled by election as provided for by law
- Staggered implementation: Positions 1-3 elected Nov 2028, Positions 4-5 elected Nov 2030, Positions 6-7 elected Nov 2032

## Legislative Passage
- Kansas Senate passed SCR 1611: **27-13** (March 6, 2025)
- Kansas House passed SCR 1611: **84-40** (March 19, 2025)
- Largely along party lines: 93% of Republicans voted FOR, Democrats voted AGAINST
- A two-thirds supermajority was required in both chambers

## Support
- Kansas Attorney General Kris Kobach
- Republican legislative majority
- Conservative groups arguing voters should have direct say in judicial selection

## Opposition

## Notes
- **CRITICAL**: This is on the AUGUST 4 PRIMARY ballot, not the November general election. Primary turnout is historically much lower, which could significantly affect the outcome.
- This is one of the most consequential ballot measures in recent Kansas history. It would fundamentally change how the state's highest court is constituted.
- The current merit-based system has been in place since 1958. Proponents say voters should choose their justices; opponents say partisan elections will politicize the judiciary.
- The 2022 Kansas abortion amendment (also on a primary ballot) saw massive turnout. This amendment could similarly drive unexpected turnout.
- This measure has direct implications for judicial independence and the balance of power between the legislature and judiciary.
